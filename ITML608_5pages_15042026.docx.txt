Detecting Sudden Price Spikes and Short-Term Corrections in Ethereum Markets Using Machine Learning: A Literature Review


Sayed Adnan Hasan Ali Jawad (2025017532), Mustafa Hasan M. Alkhenaizi (2021038763)
Sughra Hasan Maki Hasan Ali (201655794), Ayesha Siddiqa Abduljalil Ali Ahmed (20064286)
ITML 608, University of Bahrain


Abstract—This paper reviews machine learning approaches for detecting sudden price spikes and predicting short-term corrections in Ethereum markets. The review synthesizes findings across four dimensions: mean reversion evidence at intraday frequencies, event labeling methodologies, feature engineering, and model architectures. A key finding is that event labeling methodology has a considerable impact on downstream model performance than the choice of model architecture itself. However, this remains to be validated in future practical work by the research group. Volume-based features and momentum indicators emerge as the most robust predictors across independent studies, while on-chain metrics show promise but face resolution and transferability challenges and often behind a paywall. Various models such as classical ensemble models, LSTM, Transformer, and Modern Hopfield Network architectures offer tradeoffs between complexity and performance. MHNs offer a promising alternative for retrieving rare spike patterns due to their sparsity capabilities.  The review identifies a persistent disconnect between prediction accuracy and trading profitability, and proposes a two-stage pipeline combining event filtering with Triple Barrier outcome labeling as a promising framework for this task. 
Index Terms—cryptocurrency, Ethereum, price spike detection, mean reversion, event labeling, triple barrier method, machine learning, deep learning
I. INTRODUCTION
Ethereum (ETH), the second-largest cryptocurrency by market share after Bitcoin, is marked by extreme volatility. The recurring spike-then-correction events pose significant challenges for investors, and their timely detection remains an open challenge.
Market inefficiency explains the persistence of such patterns. Many studies reject the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) for cryptocurrencies [2], and Ethereum has been found to become more inefficient over time [3]. Market inefficiency increases in conditions of high volatility and low liquidity [4]. This inefficiency manifests most clearly as price overreactions: Borgards and Czudaj [5] show that overreactions occur frequently across 12 cryptocurrencies, with reversals significantly larger than those observed in the S&P 500.
Overreactions do not resolve uniformly. Caporale and Plastun [6] demonstrate that hourly returns are significantly higher on days with abnormal price movements in Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Litecoin, with prices continuing in the same direction before correcting near end-of-day. Zaremba et al. [7] show that smaller, less liquid cryptocurrencies tend to reverse, while larger coins such as Ethereum exhibit short-term momentum, making accurate identification of turning points especially valuable. Borgards and Czudaj [9] demonstrate using a random forest model that features capturing price behavior before an overreaction are the most predictive, supporting the use of Machine Learning for this task.
ML methods such as LSTM, GRU, and Gradient Boosting have been applied to forecast cryptocurrency prices [10]. However, most research frames the task as predicting whether prices will go up or down in the next few days. Very few studies have focused on spotting sudden intraday price spikes and their subsequent corrections. This paper addresses that gap. The study makes three contributions: (i) it reviews the literature on spike detection through the lens of finance and machine learning, (ii) it compares evidence on classical models, LSTM, Transformer-based models, and Modern Hopfield Networks for cryptocurrency prediction, and (iii) it synthesizes findings on features and labeling methods most relevant to Ethereum overreaction events. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows: Section II reviews related works. Section III discusses implications for system design. Section IV concludes.
II. LITERATURE REVIEW
A. Mean Reversion Patterns in Cryptocurrency Markets
A number of papers examine whether cryptocurrency prices exhibit mean reversion after sudden price movements. De Nicola [11] found significant negative autocorrelation in hourly Bitcoin returns, indicating that sharp moves are often followed by reversals. De Nicola also notes that cryptocurrency prices at intraday frequencies exhibit jump-diffusion behavior, where continuous price evolution is interrupted by sudden discrete jumps, supporting event-based rather than continuous prediction approaches. Wen et al. [12] confirmed this reversal pattern across Bitcoin, Ethereum, Litecoin, and Ripple, attributing it to investor overreaction. Borgards and Czudaj [5] found overreactions across 12 cryptocurrencies and reported that prior price momentum is the strongest predictor of whether a correction follows.
However, the effect is timeframe-dependent. Zaremba et al. [7], analyzing over 3,600 cryptocurrencies, found that liquid assets exhibit momentum at daily frequency rather than reversal. The implication is clear: reversal is concentrated at intraday frequencies, while daily and longer horizons favor trend continuation. More recent work suggests the signal persists but changes with market conditions: Pham et al. [15] found that the reversal effect shifted during the COVID-19 pandemic, and Ke et al. [16] showed in 2025 that reversal events remained predictable using data through 2024, achieving F1-scores of 0.70 for rebounds. However, because these studies span 2020-2025 and cryptocurrency markets continue to evolve, whether the intraday reversal signal persists at the same strength remains an open question.
B. Event Labeling Techniques
Applying machine learning to spike-correction prediction requires defining what constitutes an “event” in the time series data. This involves two distinct tasks: detecting when a spike occurs (event filtering), and labeling the outcome of that spike for model training (outcome labeling). These tasks can be handled by different techniques combined together, or by a single method that performs both. Gradzki et al. [13] found that choices made at the labeling stage had a larger impact on downstream model performance than the choice of model architecture — a finding established by holding models constant while varying labeling methods, and vice versa.
The most straightforward event filter is the Z-score method, which flags observations where the return exceeds a threshold number of standard deviations from a rolling mean. Its advantage is transparency — a single threshold parameter controls sensitivity. However, because it evaluates each observation independently, it cannot detect gradual buildups where several consecutive moderate moves accumulate into a significant shift [14]. The CUSUM (Cumulative Sum) method, introduced by López de Prado [14], addresses this limitation. Rather than evaluating each observation in isolation, CUSUM maintains a running total of deviations from the expected return and triggers an event when the cumulative total exceeds a threshold. This allows it to detect both sudden spikes and gradual buildups across multiple time steps. After triggering, the accumulator resets to zero, which naturally prevents event clustering during volatile periods. Gradzki et al. [13] tested CUSUM-filtered sampling against standard time-based sampling on BTC and ETH tick data and found that CUSUM filtering consistently improved results regardless of which downstream model was used.
For outcome labeling, the most widely used baseline is next-bar prediction, where every time step receives a label based on whether the following candle closes higher or lower. Gradzki et al. [13] found this approach to be inferior to structured alternatives, as it generates excessive label noise from small, insignificant price movements. The Triple Barrier Method, also introduced by López de Prado [14], provides a structured alternative that directly encodes a trade outcome. Three barriers are placed around an entry price: an upper barrier (take-profit), a lower barrier (stop-loss), and a vertical barrier (maximum holding period). The label is determined by which barrier is breached first. The barrier widths are set in units of rolling volatility rather than fixed percentages, so they automatically widen during volatile periods and tighten during calm ones [14]. Fu et al. [17] extended this approach by using genetic algorithms to optimize the barrier parameters for cryptocurrency pair trading, reporting a 51% improvement in profitability with optimized labels compared to default configurations.

	C. Feature Engineering for Spike-Correction Prediction
Once spike events are detected and labeled, the remaining challenge is predicting which spikes will correct and which will continue. The features provided to the model capture the market context at the moment of the spike. The literature identifies four primary feature categories.
Volume-based features have been consistently identified as the strongest early indicators of spike behavior. Gogol et al. [21] found volume Z-scores to be the most effective initial filter for detecting pump events in cryptocurrency markets. Avordeh et al. [22], using SHAP analysis on high-frequency Bitcoin data, reported that transaction volume accounted for 22% of total predictive power — the single largest contributor across all features tested.
Momentum indicators capture the state of the market leading up to the spike. Borgards and Czudaj [5] found that the magnitude of prior price momentum is the single strongest predictor of whether a correction will follow. Common momentum features in the literature include the Relative Strength Index (RSI), Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD), and lagged returns computed at multiple horizons.
Volatility features describe the regime in which the spike occurs. Measures such as the Garman-Klass estimator, which uses all four OHLC values per candle for more efficient volatility estimation, and Bollinger Band width, which indicates whether the market is in a compressed or expanded state, are commonly employed in the reviewed studies.
On-chain metrics represent a distinct category of features not available from standard exchange data. Li et al. [25] found that the Market Value to Realized Value (MVRV) ratio was the highest-importance feature across 12 ML models for cryptocurrency return prediction. Omole and Enke [24] used the Boruta algorithm for systematic feature selection and found MVRV and active addresses consistently dominated. For Ethereum specifically, Chen et al. [26] combined gas usage and active addresses with technical indicators in a multi-factor model spanning Q4 2021 to Q3 2024 and found that on-chain factors enhanced signal coverage during high network activity periods but weakened during low engagement. It should be noted that most on-chain feature importance research has been conducted on Bitcoin, and that on-chain data is typically available at daily resolution through free sources, creating a resolution mismatch with hourly price data.
Aslanidis et al. [23] tracked SHAP-based feature importance for Bitcoin across multiple time periods and found dramatic shifts: technological variables such as hash difficulty were the dominant predictors in 2016–2017 but became irrelevant by 2021, while Google Trends became the most important category.
D. Model Architectures
1) Classical Machine Learning Models:
Classical machine learning models remain highly effective for cryptocurrency price prediction, often matching or outperforming more advanced approaches. Adedigba et al. [27] and Sebastião and Godinho [28] show that ensemble methods such as Gradient Boosting, XGBoost, and Random Forest achieve strong performance across multiple cryptocurrencies. Adedigba et al. [27] report 67.2% accuracy with F1≈0.63, while Sebastião and Godinho [28] report success rates up to 60% using a compact feature set built mainly from lagged returns and volatility indicators. Their results suggest that well-engineered small feature sets can be as effective as larger input spaces.
Both studies also show that no single model dominates across all assets and market conditions, emphasizing the context-dependent nature of performance. Predictive accuracy alone is insufficient: Sebastião and Godinho [28] report improved Sharpe ratios under realistic evaluation conditions, including 0.5% transaction costs and no short selling, showing that financial usefulness must be judged through trading-aware evaluation.
This static training limitation motivates the exploration of sequential architectures discussed below.
2) Long Short-Term Memory Networks (LSTM):
Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks are recurrent architectures that model sequences through forget, input, and output gates that regulate the cell state [30].
Ortu et al. [30] show that LSTM performance depends strongly on feature richness. With only technical indicators, the model achieved 53.5% average hourly accuracy with high variance, but when technical indicators were combined with trading signals and social media sentiment, accuracy rose to 83% for Bitcoin and 84% for Ethereum.
Despite these stronger results, Pintelas et al. [29] highlight major limitations of standalone LSTM-based models in cryptocurrency forecasting. Even BiLSTM and CNN-LSTM did not consistently outperform classical approaches, supporting the view that cryptocurrency price dynamics can resemble a near-random walk. Performance also deteriorated under changing market regimes.
Pintelas et al. [29] found that ensemble approaches, particularly stacking, improved stability over standalone LSTM models.
3) Transformer-Based Models:
Transformer-based models can capture long-range dependencies through self-attention without recurrent processing. However, evidence in [31] shows that their performance remains highly dependent on the asset and market conditions, so greater model complexity does not guarantee better results.
Tokajuk and Chudziak [31] report that PMformer, a dual-attention model over time and feature relationships, achieved the best prediction accuracy on Bitcoin and Ethereum. Yet this higher accuracy did not translate into better trading performance; in some cases, lower-accuracy models produced higher returns and Sharpe ratios.
Transformer models were also sensitive to feature selection and market noise: smaller feature sets generalized better, whereas larger sets reduced performance. Notably, all models failed to produce consistent positive returns on Ethereum, with accuracy near random levels.
4) Modern Hopfield Networks (MHN):
Modern Hopfield Networks were introduced by Ramsauer et al. [32] as a continuous-state generalisation of classical Hopfield associative memory. Unlike the classical model, MHNs have exponentially larger storage capacity, and their update rule is mathematically equivalent to softmax attention, allowing Hopfield layers to substitute for attention, pooling, or recurrent layers in existing architectures [32].
Nicolini et al. [33] present the first application of MHN to portfolio optimisation, evaluating variants that replace LSTM and multi-head self-attention layers. Across roughly 20 years of daily returns for equities, ETFs, commodities, and Fama-French portfolios, both models matched or exceeded LSTM on ETF and stock datasets, with the Hopfield Encoder performing best on the volatile commodities dataset. Training was also faster because of lower parameter counts, though all deep learning models underperformed traditional convex optimisation on multi-regime return series [33].
Wu et al. [34] extend MHN to multivariate time series through the Sparse Tandem Hopfield Network (STanHop), which applies sparse Hopfield layers along temporal and variate axes to capture both temporal dynamics and cross-series dependencies. STanHop-Net ranked in the top two across 47 of 58 benchmark settings against FEDformer, DLinear, Informer, Autoformer, and Crossformer, and was explicitly designed to respond rapidly to rare or sudden events [34].
Auer et al. [35] pair MHN with conformal prediction in HopCPT, using the network to identify similar historical error regimes when constructing prediction intervals. The method achieved the narrowest intervals in all but one setting across multiple environmental datasets, though reliability degrades under strong distribution shift when the model fails to identify a relevant historical regime [35].

	III. DISCUSSION
A. Mean Reversion Implications
The evidence for intraday mean reversion in cryptocurrency markets is robust but bounded. Multiple studies confirm the pattern at hourly frequencies for liquid assets, and behavioral finance provides a plausible explanation through overreaction. Yet Zaremba et al. [7] show the same assets can exhibit momentum at daily frequency, implying that any spike-correction system must be designed for a specific timeframe rather than assumed to generalize across scales. For the accompanying project, this supports using hourly ETH/USDT data. More recent evidence also suggests the signal may shift as market conditions change [15], so historical results should not be treated as guarantees of future strength.
B. Event Labeling Implications
The finding that labeling methodology affected performance more than architecture choice [13] has clear implications for research priorities. Much of the literature emphasizes model comparisons while treating labeling as fixed preprocessing, but the evidence reviewed here suggests that emphasis may be misplaced. For spike-correction prediction, the Triple Barrier Method is especially suitable because its structure maps directly to continuation, correction, and timeout outcomes. Z-score and CUSUM filters play a complementary role by restricting the dataset to meaningful events before labeling. A useful direction for the accompanying project is to compare Z-score versus CUSUM filtering with Triple Barrier labeling, while recognizing that threshold choices, barrier widths, and time windows can themselves become sources of overfitting.
C. Feature Engineering Implications
Across the reviewed studies, volume-based features consistently rank as the strongest predictors of spike behavior, followed by momentum indicators. This convergence across different datasets and methods suggests these are robust signals rather than artifacts. On-chain metrics are promising for Ethereum-focused research, but two limitations remain: most feature-importance evidence comes from Bitcoin, and freely available on-chain data is usually daily rather than hourly. The temporal instability documented by Aslanidis et al. [23] is especially important, because shifting feature importance implies that models trained on one period may degrade in the next. This reinforces the need for walk-forward validation and periodic feature reassessment.
D. Model Architecture Implications
The reviewed model architectures reveal a clear tradeoff between complexity and reliability. Classical ensemble methods provide consistent baselines across assets and conditions [27], [28]. LSTM networks can achieve higher accuracy, but mainly when enriched with multi-modal inputs such as social media sentiment [30]; with technical indicators alone, performance falls toward near-random levels and becomes sensitive to regime change [29]. Transformer-based models achieve strong statistical accuracy, yet Tokajuk and Chudziak [31] show that this does not ensure trading utility, since PMformer produced the lowest prediction error while still generating negative returns on ETH. Modern Hopfield Networks remain the most conceptually intriguing direction because their ability to retrieve rare stored patterns [32] fits the nature of spike events, but they remain untested for cryptocurrency spike detection. Their practical value for intraday ETH spike-correction classification therefore remains an open empirical question.
IV. CONCLUSION
This review has examined the intersection of behavioral finance and machine learning for detecting intraday price spikes and corrections in the Ethereum market. The review confirms that while ETH exhibits momentum at daily intervals, it remains subject to significant mean-reversion and overreaction patterns at hourly frequencies. These patterns are primarily driven by speculation and market inefficiencies which are particularly pronounced during periods of global uncertainty.
The analysis reveals that robust event labeling and feature engineering contribute more to the effectiveness of a detection system than model complexity alone. Specifically, the Triple Barrier Method and CUSUM filtering provide a structured framework for modeling the discrete, jump-diffusion nature of cryptocurrency price movements that outperforms naïve next-bar predictions. Furthermore, volume and momentum indicators have been determined to be the most reliable predictors of whether a spike will result in a reversal or continued momentum. While classical ensemble methods and LSTMs form a consistent baseline, Modern Hopfield Networks present a theoretically promising but empirically untested direction for this task due to their ability to retrieve rare, associative patterns from high-dimensional data. Consequently, the accompanying project will implement a comparative study evaluating these architectures within a supervised classification framework. By bridging behavioral finance and machine learning, this research aims to present more robust tools for navigating the volatile turning points that define the Ethereum market.
